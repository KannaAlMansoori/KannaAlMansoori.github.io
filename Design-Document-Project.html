Design Document Project

An LLM-Powered Planning Agent for Academic Research and Information Gathering

Type: Team Assignment | Weighting: 20% of module mark | Word Count: 1,000 words | Deadline: End of Unit 6 | Status: ✓ Complete

The Brief

For this assignment my team was positioned as a group of software consultants specialising in agent design and development. We were asked to design an LLM-powered planning agent capable of carrying out tasks for an organisation with domain-specific requirements.

The chosen task area was academic research and information gathering. In this domain the agent receives a high-level goal, such as a research topic or query, decomposes that goal into subtasks, retrieves relevant information from online sources, processes the retrieved data through filtering, summarisation or ranking, and then stores the results in an offline location for later use.

The system had to be designed so that one or more agents operate according to their own goals, intentions or roles, with the LLM supporting planning, reasoning and task decomposition. Feasibility mattered: the design needed to be implementable using freely available technologies such as Hugging Face models, open-source libraries, or free-tier cloud services.

Agreed Success Criteria

  • The agent can identify and retrieve relevant data.
  • The data is processed in some way, for example structured, summarised or filtered.
  • The processed information is stored, saved or presented in a usable format.

What the Design Proposal Had to Cover

1. System Requirements

The libraries, models, APIs and platforms needed to build the system, including any LLMs or cloud services.

2. Design Decisions

An explanation of why particular agent architectures, planning approaches and technologies were chosen over the alternatives.

3. Development Approach

The methodology adopted to create the system and the agent-oriented techniques being leveraged.

4. Academic Rationale

A clear justification of every choice, supported by academic literature and correctly formatted references.

5. Anticipated Challenges

The difficulties expected during development, together with the paradigms, patterns or practices intended to address them. Examples include task decomposition, unreliable or noisy online data, explainability of LLM-based decisions, and managing cooperation between agents.

6. Graphical System Designs

A minimum of two different diagrams illustrating different views of the system, such as UML class, sequence or activity diagrams.

7. Tools and Environments

A clear statement of all tools, libraries, models, development environments and testing environments intended for the solution.

Grading Criteria

Criterion Weighting Focus
Knowledge and Understanding 25% Rationale supported by academic literature
Application and Understanding 25% Anticipated challenges and how they will be addressed
Criticality 25% Design diagrams, tools, libraries and environments
Presentation and Structure 25% Academic writing style, proofreading, citation formatting

Appendices could not be used to extend the core report. The document had to stand alone as a complete and concise piece of work, since one aim of the assessment was to demonstrate the ability to express ideas succinctly.

Submission Checklist

  • ✓ A bulleted list of system requirements and assumptions, design decisions and approaches, supported by academic research and references
  • ✓ A bulleted list of anticipated challenges
  • ✓ Graphical system designs using multiple diagrams
  • ✓ A bulleted list of tools, development and testing environments, libraries and models
  • ✓ Evidence of spelling and grammar checking and proofreading
  • ✓ Individual peer assessment submitted alongside the team report

Learning Outcomes Addressed

  1. Identify and critically analyse agent-based systems, differentiating between architectures and approaches.
  2. Apply and critically evaluate intelligent agent techniques to real-world problems, particularly where technical risk and uncertainty are involved.
  3. Systematically develop and demonstrate the skills required to be an effective member of a development team in a virtual professional environment, adopting real-life perspectives on team roles and organisation.
