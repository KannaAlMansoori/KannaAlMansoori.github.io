Unit 10 Seminar Preparation

Deep Learning in Action: The Future of Intelligent Technologies

Type: Seminar Preparation | Status: ✓ Complete

This seminar looks past the technical detail of deep learning and asks where these technologies are heading — and what that means ethically, socially and economically. Below is what I already know, what I plan to focus on, and where I want to push my understanding further.

What I Already Know (Earlier Units)

1. Deep Learning Learns Features, Not Rules

Unlike rule-based agents, neural networks learn their own internal representations from data. Nobody writes the rules — which is exactly why the behaviour is hard to inspect.

2. Data Quality Decides Model Quality

A model inherits whatever is in its training data, including historical bias. The maths is neutral; the dataset usually is not.

3. Accuracy Is Not the Only Metric

A model can score well overall and still fail badly for a minority group. Evaluation has to look at who the errors land on, not just how many there are.

4. Autonomy Raises Accountability Questions

From the agent units: the more independently a system acts, the harder it becomes to say who is responsible when it acts wrongly.

What to Focus On in the Seminar

1. Where Deep Learning Is Actually Deployed

Focus: Real applications — healthcare imaging, fraud detection, autonomous vehicles, language models.

Why: The gap between a benchmark result and a deployed system is where most of the risk lives.

2. The Ethical Dimension

Focus: Bias, transparency, consent, and the "black box" problem.

Why: If a model refuses a loan or flags a patient, someone has to be able to explain why.

3. The Social Dimension

Focus: Effects on work, skills, privacy, surveillance and public trust.

Why: Technology adoption is a social process, not just a technical one.

4. The Economic Dimension

Focus: Job displacement versus job creation, compute cost, and concentration of AI capability in a few large organisations.

Why: Who can afford to train these models shapes who benefits from them.

What to Enhance Understanding On

Enhance 1: Can Explainability Actually Be Achieved?

Current understanding: Deep models are hard to interpret.

Enhance: Do methods like SHAP, LIME or attention maps give real explanations, or only plausible-sounding ones?

Ask in seminar: "Is an explanation useful if the end user can't verify it?"

Enhance 2: Who Is Accountable When a Model Fails?

Current understanding: Responsibility is unclear.

Enhance: How do frameworks such as the EU AI Act assign liability between developer, deployer and user?

Ask in seminar: "In a deployed system, where does legal responsibility sit in practice?"

Enhance 3: Does Regulation Slow Innovation?

Current understanding: Regulation is usually framed as a constraint.

Enhance: Are there cases where clear rules increased adoption by building trust?

Ask in seminar: "Is there evidence that regulation helped, rather than hindered, deployment?"

Enhance 4: Environmental and Compute Cost

Current understanding: Training large models is expensive.

Enhance: How significant is the energy and water footprint, and what is being done about it?

Ask in seminar: "Should model efficiency be reported alongside accuracy?"

Quick Example: See the Difference

Rule-Based Decision

IF income > 40000 AND credit_history == "good"
    THEN approve_loan()
// Every decision can be traced to a written rule

Deep Learning Decision

decision = model.predict(applicant_features)
// Output: "decline", confidence 0.87
// Learned from 2 million past decisions — including past bias

Key difference: Rules are auditable but rigid. Deep learning is powerful but inherits whatever patterns sit in the data — which is why the ethical, social and economic questions matter as much as the accuracy score.
