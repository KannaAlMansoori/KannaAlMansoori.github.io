Units 9–11: Collaborative Discussion 3

Deep Learning

Duration: Units 9, 10, and 11 | Type: Formative | Status: In Progress

Task Overview and Learning Outcomes

Intended Learning Outcomes

  • Develop the knowledge and skills required to develop, deploy and evaluate the tools and techniques of intelligent systems to solve real-world problems

Discussion Overview

This formative discussion took place over three weeks across Units 9, 10, and 11. The activity explored the ethical implications of generative deep learning technologies, such as image generators and large language models, and considered whether these tools raise ethical concerns that organisations and users should address. Participants supported their arguments using academic literature and engaged in constructive discussions with peers.

Discussion Topic

"The advent of new technologies supported by Deep Learning models mean that it is now possible to generate 'new' content, for example, Dall-E AI to generate images or ChatGPT to create prose. Do you think that these new technologies offer any ethical issues that should be considered, and if not, why not?"

Discussion Structure

Unit 9 – Initial Post (Minimum 200 words)

  • Respond to the question of whether generative deep learning technologies raise ethical issues that should be considered
  • Discuss the key ethical concerns, including bias, ownership, misinformation, and environmental cost
  • Support the discussion with academic references using the UoEO Harvard referencing style

Unit 10 – Peer Responses (2 × 200–300 words)

  • Provide constructive feedback on at least two classmates' discussion posts
  • Focus on possible measures that could prevent the incidents and harms highlighted by peers
  • Encourage critical discussion by expanding on alternative viewpoints and practical safeguards

Unit 11 – Summary Post (Approximately 300 words)

  • Reflect on the key ideas presented in the initial post, peer feedback, and learning from the three units
  • Summarise how peer discussions influenced understanding of the ethics of generative AI
  • Highlight the practical measures needed to develop and deploy these technologies responsibly

Discussion Evidence: Forum Posts

Unit 9 – Initial Post

Topic: Ethical Issues in Generative Deep Learning Technologies

References: 3

Content

When you look at how tools like ChatGPT and DALL·E actually work, learning patterns from massive datasets and reusing them to make new text or images, I think it is hard to pretend they don't come with ethical baggage. They aren't neutral. Whatever flaws sit in the data they learned from don't just vanish. They get passed along, and often made worse.

Bias is what worries me most. These models soak up huge amounts of unfiltered internet content, so they end up repeating, and sometimes sharpening, stereotypes that are already out there (Bender et al., 2021). In something like hiring or education, that can quietly push people who are already on the margins even further back.

Then there's the question of ownership. A lot of the images and writing these models train on were taken without asking the people who made them, which raises real problems about who owns the output and whether anyone gets paid. That is exactly what several artists and writers are now fighting over in court (Dwivedi et al., 2023).

Misinformation ties into all of this. Fake but believable text and "deepfake" images can be used to trick people or twist public opinion, and that slowly wears away trust in the media (Floridi and Chiriatti, 2020). There are quieter costs too, like the sheer amount of energy and water it takes to train these systems (Bender et al., 2021).

I do find generative AI genuinely exciting. But its rewards come with responsibilities around fairness, honesty and sustainability that neither the companies building these tools nor the rest of us using them can simply ignore.

Unit 10 – Peer Response 1: Stuart

Peer's Key Argument

Stuart grounded his discussion in his own field of journalism, raising the risk of exposing anonymous sources when sensitive material is entered into public AI tools, alongside concerns about misinformation and over-reliance on AI. He also questioned whether voluntary standards are sufficient, particularly for smaller outlets.

My Response

Hi Stuart,

This is a great post, and I appreciate that you grounded it in your own field rather than talking about AI in the abstract. The point about protecting anonymous sources is one I hadn't really considered, and I think it is the most urgent of the harms you raise, so I want to focus on what could actually prevent it.

The obvious safeguard is at the tool level. A lot of the source-protection risk comes from journalists pasting sensitive material into public, cloud-based AI tools that log and retain everything. A newsroom policy of using only offline or in house models for anything touching a confidential source, plus strict data minimisation, would close much of that gap before it opens. It is prevention by workflow rather than by hoping people are careful.

On the misinformation and "laziness" problems you describe, I'd point to the Paris Charter on AI and Journalism, which argues that no journalistic content should be published without genuine human oversight, and that any significant AI involvement must be disclosed to the audience (Reporters Without Borders, 2023). Making human sign-off a formal, non-skippable step, rather than a good intention, is what stops the tempting shortcut you mention from becoming the norm.

The harder issue is the point you raise about smaller outlets, drawing on Thomson, Thomas and Matich (2025), voluntary charters only reach the newsrooms that already care. Do you think source protection is something regulation should mandate, or does that risk governments deciding how journalists work?

Key Themes Addressed

  • Acknowledged peer's field-grounded focus on protecting anonymous sources
  • Proposed a tool-level safeguard: offline/in-house models and strict data minimisation for confidential material
  • Highlighted the Paris Charter's requirement for non-skippable human oversight and disclosure
  • Raised the tension between mandating regulation and preserving journalistic independence

Unit 10 – Peer Response 2: Olusegun

Peer's Key Argument

Olusegun did not simply list the ethical issues but also proposed practical safeguards, including regular testing for bias and measures to address deepfakes, while referencing UNESCO guidance on the governance of AI.

My Response

Hi Olusegun,

I really enjoyed reading your post. What stood out to me is that you didn't just list the ethical issues, you also suggested practical safeguards, and that is the part I found most interesting. For me, the real question isn't just what the risks are, but how we can prevent them before they happen.

Take bias, for example. I agree that regular testing is important, but I also think when we test matters just as much as how we test. A lot of discrimination slips through because models are often audited only after they have already been deployed. One way to address this is by introducing better documentation before release, such as model cards, which encourage developers to explain a model's intended use, known limitations, and performance across different groups (Mitchell et al., 2019). If this became standard practice, the unfair hiring or education outcomes you mentioned would be much harder to overlook.

I also agree with your point about deepfakes. Personally, I think detection will always be trying to catch up, as Mirsky and Lee (2021) suggest. That is why I had place more emphasis on preventing the problem at its source through techniques such as digital watermarking and content provenance, together with platform policies that clearly label AI-generated content.

The point you made about UNESCO probably resonated with me the most. Even the best technical solutions won't have much impact unless organisations are required to adopt them and independent bodies ensure they are being followed. Do you think the main responsibility should lie with developers building these systems, or with regulators who set and enforce the standards?

Key Themes Addressed

  • Acknowledged peer's move from listing risks to proposing practical safeguards
  • Argued that the timing of bias testing matters, favouring pre-release documentation such as model cards
  • Emphasised preventing deepfake harm at source through watermarking, provenance, and labelling
  • Raised the question of where primary responsibility should lie: developers or regulators
