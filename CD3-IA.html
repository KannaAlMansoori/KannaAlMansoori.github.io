Units 9–11: Collaborative Discussion 3

Deep Learning

Duration: Units 9, 10, and 11 | Type: Formative | Status: ✓ Complete

Task Overview and Learning Outcomes

Module Learning Outcomes Covered

  • Learning Outcome 1: Identify and critically analyse agent-based systems, differentiating between architectures and approaches.
  • Learning Outcome 4: Systematically develop and implement the skills required to be an effective member of a development team in a virtual professional environment, adopting real-life perspectives on team roles and organisation.

Discussion Overview

This formative discussion took place over three weeks across Units 9, 10, and 11. The activity explored the ethical implications of generative deep learning technologies, such as image generators and large language models, and considered whether these tools raise ethical concerns that organisations and users should address. Participants supported their arguments using academic literature and engaged in constructive discussions with peers.

Discussion Topic

"The advent of new technologies supported by Deep Learning models mean that it is now possible to generate 'new' content, for example, Dall-E AI to generate images or ChatGPT to create prose. Do you think that these new technologies offer any ethical issues that should be considered, and if not, why not?"

Discussion Structure

Unit 9 – Initial Post (Minimum 200 words)

  • Respond to the question of whether generative deep learning technologies raise ethical issues that should be considered
  • Discuss the key ethical concerns, including bias, ownership, misinformation, and environmental cost
  • Support the discussion with academic references using the UoEO Harvard referencing style

Unit 10 – Peer Responses (2 × 200–300 words)

  • Provide constructive feedback on at least two classmates' discussion posts
  • Focus on possible measures that could prevent the incidents and harms highlighted by peers
  • Encourage critical discussion by expanding on alternative viewpoints and practical safeguards

Unit 11 – Summary Post (Approximately 300 words)

  • Reflect on the key ideas presented in the initial post, peer feedback, and learning from the three units
  • Summarise how peer discussions influenced understanding of the ethics of generative AI
  • Highlight the practical measures needed to develop and deploy these technologies responsibly

Discussion Evidence: Forum Posts

Unit 9 – Initial Post

Topic: Ethical Issues in Generative Deep Learning Technologies

References: 3

Content

When you look at how tools like ChatGPT and DALL·E actually work, learning patterns from massive datasets and reusing them to make new text or images, I think it is hard to pretend they don't come with ethical baggage. They aren't neutral. Whatever flaws sit in the data they learned from don't just vanish. They get passed along, and often made worse.

Bias is what worries me most. These models soak up huge amounts of unfiltered internet content, so they end up repeating, and sometimes sharpening, stereotypes that are already out there (Bender et al., 2021). In something like hiring or education, that can quietly push people who are already on the margins even further back.

Then there's the question of ownership. A lot of the images and writing these models train on were taken without asking the people who made them, which raises real problems about who owns the output and whether anyone gets paid. That is exactly what several artists and writers are now fighting over in court (Dwivedi et al., 2023).

Misinformation ties into all of this. Fake but believable text and "deepfake" images can be used to trick people or twist public opinion, and that slowly wears away trust in the media (Floridi and Chiriatti, 2020). There are quieter costs too, like the sheer amount of energy and water it takes to train these systems (Bender et al., 2021).

I do find generative AI genuinely exciting. But its rewards come with responsibilities around fairness, honesty and sustainability that neither the companies building these tools nor the rest of us using them can simply ignore.

Unit 10 – Peer Response 1: Stuart

Peer's Key Argument

Stuart grounded his discussion in his own field of journalism, arguing that generative AI risks misinformation, missed newsworthiness and inherited bias. He raised the protection of anonymous sources as a less-discussed concern, since increasingly capable AI analysis tools threaten a core journalistic principle, and noted that smaller outlets lack editorial guidelines yet may be more likely to use these tools. He concluded on a balanced note, arguing that AI need not end good journalism if used with proper review.

My Response

Hi Stuart,

This is a great post, and I appreciate that you grounded it in your own field rather than talking about AI in the abstract. The point about protecting anonymous sources is one I hadn't really considered, and I think it is the most urgent of the harms you raise, so I want to focus on what could actually prevent it.

The obvious safeguard is at the tool level. A lot of the source-protection risk comes from journalists pasting sensitive material into public, cloud-based AI tools that log and retain everything. A newsroom policy of using only offline or in house models for anything touching a confidential source, plus strict data minimisation, would close much of that gap before it opens. It is prevention by workflow rather than by hoping people are careful.

On the misinformation and "laziness" problems you describe, I'd point to the Paris Charter on AI and Journalism, which argues that no journalistic content should be published without genuine human oversight, and that any significant AI involvement must be disclosed to the audience (Reporters Without Borders, 2023). Making human sign-off a formal, non-skippable step, rather than a good intention, is what stops the tempting shortcut you mention from becoming the norm.

The harder issue is the point you raise about smaller outlets, drawing on Thomson, Thomas and Matich (2025), voluntary charters only reach the newsrooms that already care. Do you think source protection is something regulation should mandate, or does that risk governments deciding how journalists work?

Unit 10 – Peer Response 2: Olusegun

Peer's Key Argument

Olusegun did not simply list the ethical issues — bias, copyright, misinformation and deepfakes — but also proposed practical safeguards, including regular bias testing, human oversight, transparent use of data, clear labelling of AI-generated content, and routes to challenge harmful outputs. Drawing on UNESCO, he argued that AI development has outpaced policy and regulatory frameworks, supporting the need for accountability and independent oversight.

My Response

Hi Olusegun,

I really enjoyed reading your post. What stood out to me is that you didn't just list the ethical issues, you also suggested practical safeguards, and that is the part I found most interesting. For me, the real question isn't just what the risks are, but how we can prevent them before they happen.

Take bias, for example. I agree that regular testing is important, but I also think when we test matters just as much as how we test. A lot of discrimination slips through because models are often audited only after they have already been deployed. One way to address this is by introducing better documentation before release, such as model cards, which encourage developers to explain a model's intended use, known limitations, and performance across different groups (Mitchell et al., 2019). If this became standard practice, the unfair hiring or education outcomes you mentioned would be much harder to overlook.

I also agree with your point about deepfakes. Personally, I think detection will always be trying to catch up, as Mirsky and Lee (2021) suggest. That is why I would place more emphasis on preventing the problem at its source through techniques such as digital watermarking and content provenance, together with platform policies that clearly label AI-generated content.

The point you made about UNESCO probably resonated with me the most. Even the best technical solutions won't have much impact unless organisations are required to adopt them and independent bodies ensure they are being followed. Do you think the main responsibility should lie with developers building these systems, or with regulators who set and enforce the standards?

Unit 11 – Summary Post

Synthesis of Initial Post, Peer Feedback, and Units 9-11 Learning

In my initial post I argued that tools like ChatGPT and DALL·E are not really neutral. The problems in the data they learn from, such as bias, unlicensed content and the risk of misinformation, get passed into their outputs and are often made worse (Bender et al., 2021).

After reading more on the topic and thinking about the feedback from Pekarova, my view has shifted from just naming these problems to looking at how they can actually be managed.

I still believe unfiltered web data feeds stereotypes. What changed is how I see the fix. It needs to be an ongoing process, with careful dataset curation and regular bias checks throughout the model's life, as the NIST framework suggests (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023). On ownership, the disputes raised by Dwivedi et al. (2023) show why data provenance matters, and this is now a legal duty for large model providers under the EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, 2024).

On misinformation, I first focused on lost trust (Floridi and Chiriatti, 2020), but I would now add watermarking and clear labelling as practical fixes the AI Act also requires.

Pekarova's response also reminded me that these tools are already being used in real settings like healthcare, education and finance. This is why I agree that technical controls only work if people stay involved and keep proper oversight in high-risk cases. Thinking further ahead, it seems clear that building in fairness and honesty now is much easier than trying to add them later.

So my main view has not changed, but it feels more grounded. Generative AI is exciting and useful, yet its benefits depend on good data, proper checks and human oversight being planned in from the start, not just assumed.

Critically Analysing Deep Learning Systems

What I did: In my initial post I argued that generative models are not neutral tools. Because they learn patterns from large, unfiltered datasets, whatever is wrong in that data, bias, unlicensed content, false information, is carried into the output and sometimes made stronger (Bender et al., 2021).

Comparing approaches to the problem: The discussion pushed me to compare different ways of dealing with these risks, and I found that when a control is applied matters as much as what the control is. Testing a model for bias after it is already deployed catches harm late; documentation before release, such as model cards, catches it earlier (Mitchell et al., 2019). The same pattern appears with deepfakes. Detection is always chasing the newest generator (Mirsky and Lee, 2021), so watermarking and content provenance, which act at the point of creation, are the stronger approach. I also compared voluntary standards with binding ones: the Paris Charter depends on newsrooms choosing to follow it (Reporters Without Borders, 2023), while the EU AI Act makes provenance and labelling a legal duty (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, 2024).

What I concluded: My position did not reverse, but it became more practical. I moved from listing ethical problems to identifying the mechanisms that manage them across a model's whole life cycle, as set out in the NIST risk management framework (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023). Technical controls alone are not enough. They only work when human oversight is required and someone independent checks that it is happening.

Working in a Virtual Team

What I did: I wrote the initial post, replied to two classmates in Unit 10, and wrote a summary in Unit 11 that brought their contributions together with my own.

How I worked with others: I tried to add something useful to each reply rather than just agreeing. With Stuart I focused on the one harm he raised that I thought was most urgent, protecting anonymous sources, and suggested a workflow-level safeguard instead of relying on people being careful. With Olusegun I built on his safeguards by arguing that the timing of bias testing matters. I ended both replies with a question so the discussion could continue, and both led to further exchange. The feedback also changed my own thinking: Stuart raised a professional risk I had never considered, and Pekarova reminded me that these tools are already in use in healthcare, education and finance, which is what moved me from abstract ethics towards practical oversight.

Reflection

At the start of this discussion I was confident about naming the ethical problems with generative AI. What I could not do was say what should actually be done about them. By Unit 11 I could point to specific mechanisms, dataset curation, model cards, watermarking, provenance and independent oversight, and explain where each one fits in a model's life cycle.

Reading outside the module helped, but the peer replies helped more. Stuart works in journalism and raised a risk I would never have thought of, and that showed me how much easier it is to see a problem clearly when someone brings their own professional context to it.

Coming from a statistics background, the part that stayed with me is that fairness and honesty are much easier to build in at the start than to add later. In future work I will treat data quality, documentation and oversight as design decisions rather than as checks to run at the end.
