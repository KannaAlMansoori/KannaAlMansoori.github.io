Unit 12 Seminar Preparation

The Future of Intelligent Agents

Type: Seminar Preparation | Status: ✓ Complete

This final seminar pulls together everything from the module and asks where intelligent technologies are going next. We will discuss the technologies we wrote about in Unit 10 with the rest of the class. Below is what I already know, what I want to focus on, and where I want to push my understanding further.

What I Already Know (Earlier Units)

1. Agents Act, Models Predict

From the early units: an agent perceives, decides and acts on its own. A deep learning model only produces an output. The interesting future systems are the ones that combine both.

2. Communication Is the Hard Part

From CD2: KQML and FIPA-ACL showed that getting agents to talk is easy, but getting them to mean the same thing is not. Today's LLM-based multi-agent systems face the same problem.

3. Capability Grows Faster Than Governance

From CD3: what a system can do usually arrives before the rules about what it should do. The NIST framework and the EU AI Act are attempts to close that gap.

4. Autonomy Shifts Responsibility

A theme running through the whole module: the more independently a system acts, the harder it becomes to say who is accountable when it gets something wrong.

What to Focus On in the Seminar

1. Appraising Today's Technologies

Focus: What current systems actually do well, and where they still fail.

Why: Any prediction about the future has to start from an honest view of the present.

2. Where the Technology Is Heading

Focus: Agentic AI, multi-step task execution, tool use, and systems that plan over longer horizons.

Why: This is the direction the module has been building towards: agents that do, not just answer.

3. Science Fiction or Realistic Projection?

Focus: Separating what current trends support from what is speculation.

Why: The seminar asks whether deep learning is moving into territory once thought fictional. That needs evidence, not enthusiasm.

4. Ethical and Social Ramifications

Focus: Work, oversight, concentration of capability, and public trust.

Why: These consequences arrive with the technology, not after it.

What to Enhance Understanding On

Enhance 1: Does More Autonomy Mean Less Oversight?

Current understanding: Human oversight is the standard safeguard.

Enhance: Can oversight keep working when agents run long, multi-step tasks that a person cannot follow in real time?

Ask in seminar: "What does meaningful human oversight look like when the system moves faster than the reviewer?"

Enhance 2: Do Old Communication Problems Return?

Current understanding: KQML's semantic problems were addressed by FIPA-ACL.

Enhance: Modern agents coordinate through natural language, which has no formal semantics at all. Is this a step backwards?

Ask in seminar: "Have we solved the ontology problem, or just stopped defining it?"

Enhance 3: Who Gets to Build These Systems?

Current understanding: Training large models is expensive.

Enhance: If capability concentrates in a few organisations, what happens to smaller institutions and to countries outside that group?

Ask in seminar: "Does the cost of compute decide who benefits from AI?"

Enhance 4: What Changes for My Own Field?

Current understanding: Agents can support data cleaning, pattern detection and modelling.

Enhance: If agents carry out full analytical workflows, what does the statistician's role become?

Ask in seminar: "Which parts of professional judgement should stay human by design?"

Quick Example: How the Role Changes

Today: The Model Assists

analyst → asks model → gets a suggested method
analyst → checks it → runs it → interprets the result
// The human holds every decision point

Emerging: The Agent Executes

analyst → states a goal → agent plans the steps
agent → cleans data → selects method → runs → reports
// The human reviews at the end, not at each step

Key difference: The work does not disappear; it moves. The human role shifts from carrying out the steps to setting the goal and judging the result. That is where the ethical and social questions in this seminar come from — oversight only counts if the reviewer can still tell whether the answer is right.
