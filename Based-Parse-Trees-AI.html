Constituency-Based Parse Trees

Title: Constituency Parsing of Natural Language Sentences | Module: Understanding Artificial Intelligence | Type: Formative Activity | Status: ✓ Complete

Learning Outcomes

  • The knowledge and skills required to develop, deploy and evaluate the tools and techniques of intelligent systems to solve real-world problems.
  • An understanding of contemporary research issues in the area of intelligent agent systems.
  • Understand how phrase-structure grammar decomposes natural language sentences into hierarchical constituents.
  • Recognise and analyse structural ambiguity in natural language and its implications for automated parsing.

Activity Overview

This activity focuses on constituency parsing, a foundational technique in natural language processing (NLP). A constituency-based parse tree breaks a sentence into nested constituents — groups of words that behave as single grammatical units — using phrase-structure grammar. The core rule is S → NP VP: every sentence (S) divides into a subject noun phrase (NP) and a predicate verb phrase (VP), which are then broken down further until every word is assigned a part of speech.

Task:

Create a constituency-based parse tree for each of the following phrases and add the completed trees to the e-Portfolio:

  1. The government raised interest rates.
  2. The internet gives everyone a voice.
  3. The man saw the dog with the telescope.

Grammar Labels Used:

  • S — Sentence
  • NP — Noun Phrase
  • VP — Verb Phrase
  • PP — Prepositional Phrase
  • Det — Determiner, N — Noun, V — Verb, P — Preposition, Pron — Pronoun

Why Constituency Parsing?

Constituency parsing is how intelligent agents move from a flat string of words to a structured representation of meaning. Applications include:

  • Machine translation, where structure must be preserved across languages
  • Question answering and information extraction
  • Grammar checking and text-to-speech prosody
  • Any task where an agent must interpret human language correctly

Tree 1: The government raised interest rates.

Parse Tree

This figure shows the sentence decomposed into a subject NP ("The government") and a VP ("raised interest rates"), with the object NP "interest rates" nested inside the verb phrase.

Interpretation - Tree 1

A straightforward S → NP VP structure. The subject NP "The government" consists of a determiner (Det: "The") and a noun (N: "government"). The VP consists of the verb (V: "raised") and the object NP "interest rates".

Because "interest rates" is a compound noun, "interest" sits inside the object NP as a noun modifier of "rates" (N + N), rather than as a separate constituent. This illustrates how phrase-structure grammar handles noun–noun modification without introducing a new phrase type.

Tree 2: The internet gives everyone a voice.

Parse Tree

This figure shows a ditransitive verb phrase in which the VP branches into two noun phrases — the indirect object "everyone" and the direct object "a voice".

Interpretation - Tree 2

The subject NP "The internet" (Det + N) combines with the VP "gives everyone a voice". The key feature here is that "gives" is a ditransitive verb — it takes two objects. As a result, the VP branches into two noun phrases: the indirect object NP "everyone" (a pronoun) and the direct object NP "a voice" (Det + N).

This shows that the internal structure of a verb phrase depends on the argument requirements of the verb. A transitive verb would take one object NP; an intransitive verb would take none; a ditransitive verb such as "gives" takes two.

Tree 3: The man saw the dog with the telescope.

This sentence is deliberately ambiguous and is a classic example of PP-attachment ambiguity. The prepositional phrase "with the telescope" can attach in two different places in the tree, producing two valid structures and two distinct meanings.

Reading A — Telescope is the Instrument (PP under VP)

Here the prepositional phrase "with the telescope" attaches to the verb phrase, meaning the man used the telescope to see the dog (the telescope is the instrument of seeing).

Reading B — The Dog Has the Telescope (PP under NP)

Here the prepositional phrase attaches to the noun phrase "the dog", meaning the dog is the one holding the telescope (the PP modifies the object noun).

Interpretation - Tree 3

Both trees are grammatically correct; the difference lies entirely in where the PP attaches:

  • Reading A (PP under VP): The PP is a sibling of the object NP inside the verb phrase, describing how the seeing happened.
  • Reading B (PP under NP): The PP is nested inside the object NP, describing which dog was seen.

This ambiguity cannot be resolved by grammar alone — both structures are legal. A human uses world knowledge and context to prefer Reading A (telescopes are more plausibly held by people than dogs). For an intelligent system, this is a genuine challenge, which connects directly to contemporary research in NLP.

Overall Analysis and Key Findings

1. Structure Follows Grammatical Rules

What I Found: All three sentences share the same top-level rule S → NP VP, yet their internal structures differ significantly based on the verbs and phrases involved.

What This Means:

Phrase-structure grammar provides a small set of recursive rules that generate an enormous variety of sentence structures. The subject–predicate split is universal, but the shape of the verb phrase adapts to the verb's requirements — a single object for "raised", two objects for the ditransitive "gives", and an object plus an optional prepositional phrase for "saw".

2. Compound Nouns and Verb Arguments

What I Found: "interest rates" behaves as a single compound noun (N + N inside one NP), while "gives" forces two separate object NPs.

What This Means:

Correct parsing requires knowledge that goes beyond individual words. A parser must know that "interest" modifies "rates" rather than being a separate object, and that "gives" is ditransitive. This lexical knowledge is exactly what modern statistical and neural parsers learn from large annotated corpora such as the Penn Treebank.

3. Structural Ambiguity as a Research Problem

What I Found: Sentence 3 produces two equally valid parse trees depending on PP attachment.

What This Means:

PP-attachment ambiguity is one of the most studied problems in computational linguistics. Because grammar alone cannot decide between the readings, intelligent systems rely on probabilistic parsing (choosing the most likely tree given training data) and contextual cues to disambiguate. This is a live area of research, particularly with the rise of neural and transformer-based language models that resolve ambiguity using learned context rather than hand-written rules — directly addressing the learning outcome on contemporary research in intelligent agent systems.

Learning Outcomes Achieved

  • Applied phrase-structure grammar: Successfully decomposed three natural-language sentences into hierarchical constituency parse trees using the rule S → NP VP and correct part-of-speech tagging.
  • Identified verb argument structures: Distinguished transitive, ditransitive, and instrument-taking verbs, and showed how each shapes the verb phrase differently.
  • Handled compound nouns: Correctly represented "interest rates" as a compound noun within a single NP rather than as separate constituents.
  • Analysed structural ambiguity: Produced two valid parse trees for an ambiguous sentence and explained the meaning associated with each PP-attachment.
  • Connected theory to intelligent systems: Related constituency parsing to real-world NLP applications and to the tools and techniques used by intelligent agents to interpret human language.
  • Engaged with contemporary research: Recognised PP-attachment ambiguity as an active research problem addressed by probabilistic and neural parsing methods.
  • Communicated results clearly: Presented parse trees, summaries, and interpretations in a structured, academic format suitable for an e-Portfolio.

Reflection: Learning, Growth, and Development

This activity gave me a hands-on understanding of how natural language can be represented as structured data. Drawing the parse trees by hand made me think about why words group the way they do, rather than treating a sentence as a flat sequence of words.

The three examples showed how the single rule S → NP VP underlies very different sentences. The second sentence taught me that a verb's argument structure shapes the whole predicate — recognising that "gives" is ditransitive made it clear "everyone" and "a voice" are two separate objects, not one.

The third sentence was the most valuable. Seeing that one sentence can have two correct trees showed me that ambiguity is the central challenge of natural language understanding, not an edge case. Humans resolve "The man saw the dog with the telescope" using world knowledge, but an intelligent agent must learn this from data. This connects to contemporary research: classical parsers used probabilistic grammars to rank competing trees, while modern transformer-based models resolve ambiguity through learned context.

Overall, I developed the ability to build parse trees, tag parts of speech, and communicate a technical analysis clearly. To extend this, I would compare my trees with automatic parsers such as spaCy or NLTK, and explore dependency parsing. The activity reinforced a theme across my MSc: intelligent systems succeed by recovering structure from messy real-world input before they can reason or act.
